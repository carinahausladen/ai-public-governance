# AI and Public Governance — Syllabus

**Core question:** How should governments, public institutions, and civil society govern, deploy, and respond to increasingly capable AI systems?

A politics / public-administration course first, an AI course second. 12 weeks, six modules.

**Course premise.** A recurring claim of this course is that AI companies often move first and states respond later. New models, products, partnerships, access programs, and safety frameworks are usually introduced by firms on their own timeline. Governments then react through procurement, regulation, litigation, guidance, public controversy, or institutional catch-up. The course tests that claim across cases rather than assuming it is always true.

**Proseminar II focus.** This version is designed as a Proseminar II. Alongside the substantive study of AI and public governance, students develop an individual research project that culminates in a short exposé and a detailed exposé. In response to the programme emphasis on `Datenerhebung und -analyse`, students build a small empirical project using course materials such as policy documents, media coverage, lab self-presentation, or a small comparative set of public-sector AI cases.

**Learning objectives.** By the end, students can: (1) understand what modern AI can and cannot do; (2) analyze the public-policy implications of AI; (3) evaluate AI use in government and public services; (4) assess risks, governance mechanisms, and regulatory approaches; (5) design practical public-sector AI interventions; (6) critically interpret claims made by AI developers, distinguishing evidence from positioning; (7) translate a substantive course question into a feasible small empirical research design using documents, media sources, or comparative cases.

> **Reading the sources critically.** Readings are tagged by type — **[Lab]** = AI-developer self-presentation (product/PR/lab research); **[News]** = journalism; **[Policy]** = government/official; **[Platform]** = social/practitioner post. Lab and platform items are primary evidence of how actors frame themselves, not neutral fact — read them for *what is claimed* and *what the framing omits*.

## Running activity — Frontier AI Governance Tracker

Each week, students monitor: [OpenAI Newsroom](https://openai.com/news/) · [Anthropic News](https://www.anthropic.com/news) · [OECD AI Observatory](https://oecd.ai) · [Stanford HAI](https://hai.stanford.edu)

At least twice during the term, one tracker entry should be a **country case**. Switzerland is the recommended starting case because it shows public-sector AI through infrastructure, staffing, transparency, and administrative coordination rather than only through headline regulation.

For one development, each student submits: **(1)** the development, **(2)** a governance implication, **(3)** a policy recommendation, **(4)** a critical question about how the source frames it, and **(5)** the source type (lab / news / policy / platform).

Expected format: 250 to 350 words per week.

These weekly entries also function as a small empirical source base from which students can develop their exposés.

## Major assignments

### Presentation / project colloquium

One presentation on the student's developing research project.

- 10 to 15 minutes
- should present the research question, case or corpus, and planned mode of analysis
- can be organized as part of one or two colloquium-style sessions with peer feedback

### Short exposé / sketch

One short exposé or project sketch for the seminar paper.

- approximately 2 pages plus bibliography
- should include the research question, motivation, tentative conceptual framework, chosen case or corpus, planned data or materials, and analysis strategy

Possible project types include a live governance controversy, a comparative governance analysis, or an institutional design project grounded in empirical cases.

### Detailed exposé / seminar paper

One detailed exposé that develops the research question, conceptual framework, and empirical design in full.

- 10 to 12 pages
- should elaborate the research question, situate it in the debate, justify the case or corpus selection, and specify how data or materials will be collected and analyzed
- should draw on at least two modules
- can use the weekly tracker as part of its empirical source base
