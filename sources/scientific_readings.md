# Scientific Readings for the Syllabus

Compiled on 2026-05-29.

This file proposes **one best-fit scientific paper for each week** of the syllabus. I prioritized papers that are:

- analytically strong;
- teachable in a politics / public-administration course;
- closely matched to the weekly theme;
- drawn from primary academic sources such as journals, NBER, SSRN, or arXiv.

This is not a list of the "most famous AI papers." It is a list of the papers most useful for **this course**.

---

## Module 1 — Foundations: What AI Can and Cannot Do

### Week 1 — Capabilities, limits, and the pace of change

- [GPTs are GPTs: An Early Look at the Labor Market Impact Potential of Large Language Models](https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.10130) — Tyna Eloundou, Sam Manning, Pamela Mishkin, Daniel Rock (2023, arXiv)

Why this is the best fit:
- It gives students a concrete way to think about what LLMs can do across tasks, not just anecdotes about impressive outputs.
- It is especially useful for distinguishing **capability**, **exposure**, and **actual adoption**.
- It also sets up later discussions of labor markets, governance, and public-sector implications.

### Week 2 — Augmentation vs. automation; reading capability claims

- [Experimental Evidence on the Productivity Effects of Generative Artificial Intelligence](https://doi.org/10.1126/science.adh2586) — Shakked Noy, Whitney Zhang (2023, *Science*)

Why this is the best fit:
- It is one of the clearest early experimental papers on what generative AI actually does in practice.
- The findings map directly onto the week’s core distinction between **augmentation** and **automation**.
- It is also pedagogically strong because it shows productivity gains, inequality effects, and behavioral spillovers without requiring a technical background.

## Module 2 — AI and the State

### Week 3 — Public-sector adoption and digital government

- [Algorithmic Government: Automating Public Services and Supporting Civil Servants in using Data Science Technologies](https://academic.oup.com/comjnl/article/62/3/448/5070384) — Zeynep Engin, Philip Treleaven (2019, *The Computer Journal*)

Why this is the best fit:
- It gives a broad conceptual map of how AI enters government.
- It works well as a bridge between digital government, public services, and administrative transformation.
- It is especially useful early in the module because it treats AI as a governance and organizational issue, not just a technical tool.

### Week 4 — Procurement, vendor dependence, administrative capacity

- [Human–AI Interactions in Public Sector Decision Making: “Automation Bias” and “Selective Adherence” to Algorithmic Advice](https://academic.oup.com/jpart/article/33/1/153/6524536) — Saar Alon-Barkat, Madalina Busuioc (2023, *Journal of Public Administration Research and Theory*)

Why this is the best fit:
- It brings the public sector back in at the level of actual decision-making.
- It is especially strong for this week because it connects AI adoption to **bureaucratic judgment**, **bias**, and **institutional capacity**.
- It also pairs very well with real policy scandals and procurement cases in the syllabus.

## Module 3 — AI and Democracy

### Week 5 — Misinformation and elections

- [On the way to deep fake democracy? Deep fakes in election campaigns in 2023](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/european-political-science/article/on-the-way-to-deep-fake-democracy-deep-fakes-in-election-campaigns-in-2023/8F97B6AD4C40B195B369696926B5F7EB) — Jędrzej Skrzypczak, et al. (2025, *European Political Science*)

Why this is the best fit:
- It is directly about elections, deepfakes, and democratic trust.
- It avoids pure technological alarmism and instead asks how people, institutions, and media actually respond.
- That makes it a very good match for your course’s political framing.

### Week 6 — Political persuasion and government communication

- [A meta-analysis of the persuasive power of large language models](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-30783-y) — Lukas Hölbling, Sebastian Maier, Stefan Feuerriegel (2025, *Scientific Reports*)

Why this is the best fit:
- It directly addresses whether LLMs can persuade as effectively as humans.
- It is ideal for a week on political persuasion because it moves beyond speculation and synthesizes the available evidence.
- It also helps students think more carefully about where the real democratic risks lie: not generic hype, but context-specific persuasive capacity.

## Module 4 — AI Policy and Regulation

### Week 7 — Risk-based regulation and the EU AI Act

- [Artificial intelligence co-regulation? The role of standards in the EU AI Act](https://academic.oup.com/ijlit/article/doi/10.1093/ijlit/eaae011/7709069) — Linda Jäck, Robert Kilian, Dominik Ebel (2024, *International Journal of Law and Information Technology*)

Why this is the best fit:
- It gets students past the headline that the EU AI Act is simply "risk-based regulation."
- It shows how standards bodies and technical implementation become part of governance.
- That makes it especially valuable for a public-governance course, because it highlights where real authority sits after legislation is passed.

### Week 8 — U.S. approaches, federalism, and private self-regulation

- [Federalism & Algorithms](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=5290048) — David S. Rubenstein (2025, SSRN / forthcoming *Arizona Law Review*)

Why this is the best fit:
- It is the clearest match for the U.S. federalism question in the syllabus.
- It helps students understand why AI governance in the United States often looks fragmented, contested, and patchwork-like.
- It also connects naturally to debates over pre-emption, state experimentation, and the limits of federal coordination.

## Module 5 — AI for Public Good

### Week 9 — Education

- [AI tutoring outperforms in-class active learning: an RCT introducing a novel research-based design in an authentic educational setting](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-97652-6) — Louis-Philippe Beland, Alex Kim, et al. (2025, *Scientific Reports*)

Why this is the best fit:
- It gives you a randomized controlled trial rather than a general opinion piece on AI in education.
- It is highly teachable because it asks a concrete question: what happens when AI becomes part of learning design?
- It also supports the course’s broader theme that educational AI is not just pedagogy, but infrastructure and institutional choice.

### Week 10 — Nonprofits and the distribution of benefits

- [Applying AI to Rebuild Middle Class Jobs](https://www.nber.org/papers/w32140) — David Autor (2024, NBER Working Paper)

Why this is the best fit:
- This is one of the most teachable papers on the distributional politics of AI.
- It asks whether AI can complement workers rather than simply replace them, which is exactly the right question for a week on public benefit and distribution.
- It also gives students a more normatively ambitious framework than standard "AI raises productivity" accounts.

## Module 6 — Future Governance

### Week 11 — Institutional design and lab self-governance

- [Public Constitutional AI](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=4874670) — Gilad Abiri (2024/2025, SSRN / forthcoming *Georgia Law Review*)

Why this is the best fit:
- It is a strong normative and institutional-design paper rather than a vague ethics essay.
- It fits the week well because it asks how frontier AI systems could acquire democratic legitimacy rather than merely private alignment.
- It also gives students a concrete institutional proposal they can argue with.

### Week 12 — Frontier governance, civil-military bargaining, contracts as governance

- [Regulating Transformative Technologies](https://www.nber.org/papers/w31461) — Daron Acemoglu, Todd Lensman (2023, NBER Working Paper)

Why this is the best fit:
- It is one of the strongest high-level papers on how society should regulate powerful general-purpose technologies under uncertainty.
- It fits the final week because it links innovation, risk, timing, and gradual adoption.
- It also helps students think beyond individual scandals toward the core governance problem: how to regulate powerful systems before harms are fully visible.

## Suggested use

- If you want **one scientific anchor reading per week**, the list above is ready to use.
- If you want **two papers per week**, the next step should be to add one complementary reading beside each primary paper:
  - one empirical + one normative, or
  - one public-administration + one law/policy, depending on the week.

---

## Bottom line

The strongest academic pattern mirrors the course premise:

- AI capability and product change often arrive first;
- institutions respond through adoption, regulation, standards, and organizational redesign;
- the hardest governance questions concern timing, legitimacy, accountability, and distribution, not only technical performance.
