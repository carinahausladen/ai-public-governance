## Module 3: AI and Democracy

### 5. The Guardian: chatbot misinformation before the Scottish election

- Title: [ChatGPT and other AI bots made huge errors before Scottish election, study finds](https://www.theguardian.com/technology/2026/may/20/ai-chatbots-chatgpt-replika-grok-gemini-misinformation-scottish-election-demos)
- Date: 2026-05-20
- Retrieval status: article body available
- Fetched content:
  - The article says the Electoral Commission called for stronger legal controls after Demos tested five free AI tools on 75 election questions.
  - Demos found misinformation in 34% of answers overall.
  - The reported failures included invented scandals, wrong election dates, false voter-ID claims, and misplaced candidates.
  - The article also reports polling suggesting that 20% of voters had used AI chatbots or search tools for election information.
- Teaching summary:
  - This is one of the strongest pieces for your democracy module.
  - It connects misinformation, platform design, voter behavior, and the institutional question of who should regulate AI-generated political information.

### 6. The Guardian: deepfake detection before elections

- Title: [Software tackling deepfakes to be piloted for Scottish and Welsh elections](https://www.theguardian.com/technology/2026/jan/08/pilot-software-tackle-deepfakes-scottish-welsh-elections)
- Date: 2026-01-08
- Retrieval status: article body available
- Fetched content:
  - The article reports that election officials were working with the Home Office on a pilot to detect AI-generated deepfake videos and images.
  - The detection tools were expected to be operational before late-March campaigning.
  - The Electoral Commission was also seeking stronger takedown powers for hoax content on platforms.
- Teaching summary:
  - This is an excellent institutional-response case.
  - It lets students compare preventive governance, platform moderation, and state capacity for real-time election protection.

### 7. The Guardian: political deepfakes and propaganda

- Title: [‘They feel true’: political deepfakes are growing in influence – even if people know they aren’t real](https://www.theguardian.com/technology/2026/mar/28/military-deepfakes-ai-propaganda-money)
- Date: 2026-03-28
- Retrieval status: article body available
- Fetched content:
  - The article argues that creators are not only faking public figures but also fabricating entirely synthetic people for political and military-themed propaganda.
  - It links these images to attention, monetization, and belief reinforcement rather than only deception in the narrow sense.
- Teaching summary:
  - This is a strong reading for political persuasion.
  - It moves students beyond the simple "fake or real" frame and toward questions about ideology, affect, and the economics of synthetic political media.

### 8. Washington Post: White House use of AI imagery

- Title: [Trump's use of AI images pushes new boundaries, further eroding public trust, experts say](https://www.washingtonpost.com/politics/2026/01/27/ai-videos-trump-ice-artificial-intelligence/059bcf28-fb92-11f0-954b-b80c7ed67fc7_story.html/)
- Date: 2026-01-27
- Retrieval status: headline and search snippet only
- Fetched content:
  - The piece describes concern over the White House's use of AI-edited imagery.
  - The reporting frames this as a trust problem, especially when government communication itself blurs the line between documentation, persuasion, and fabrication.
- Teaching summary:
