# Switzerland: AI in the Public Sector

Compiled on 2026-05-29.

This note gathers public evidence on how AI is being adopted, governed, and staffed in the Swiss public sector. It is especially useful for the course claim that companies often move first while public institutions respond through local infrastructure, guidance, procurement, and new administrative roles.

## Why this case matters

Switzerland is a strong teaching case because it shows AI governance at multiple levels at once:

- **city level**: operational deployment inside government;
- **cantonal level**: project transparency, guidance, and administrative experimentation;
- **federal level**: coordination, strategy, and regulatory catch-up;
- **ecosystem level**: new public-sector-facing AI jobs around implementation, delivery, and governance.

---

## 1. City of Zurich: a secure internal AI assistant

### ZüriA

Source:
- [Stadt Zürich lanciert eigene, sichere KI-Assistenz](https://www.stadt-zuerich.ch/de/aktuell/medienmitteilungen/2026/03/zuerich-lanciert-eigene-sichere-ki-assistenz.html) (City of Zurich, 2026-03)

Key points:
- The City of Zurich says employees now have access to **"ZüriA"**, an internal AI assistant.
- It is designed to process **internal, confidential, and strictly confidential** information.
- The system is run in **city-operated data centers**.
- The city says it uses **open-source language models** rather than relying only on public commercial tools.
- Current uses include summarizing documents, drafting messages, editing text, and translation.

Why it matters:
- This is a strong public-sector case of **local infrastructure + data sovereignty**.
- It is not just "using ChatGPT"; it is a case of a city building a governed internal AI layer for administrative work.
- It also complicates the simple story of state dependence on firms: the state is still using the generative-AI paradigm, but it is trying to regain control over hosting, security, and workflow integration.

## 2. Canton of Zurich: project transparency and a governance guide

Source:
- [Künstliche Intelligenz in der Verwaltung](https://www.zh.ch/de/politik-staat/kanton/kantonale-verwaltung/digitale-verwaltung/kuenstliche-intelligenz.html) (Canton of Zurich, accessed 2026-05)

Key points:
- The canton explicitly says it uses AI and provides a **public-facing list of projects**.
- It also highlights a **guide for public administration** developed with the University of Basel and AlgorithmWatch Switzerland.
- The canton is working on an **AES register** for "Algorithmic Decision Systems" to increase transparency.

Examples of listed projects:
- plausibility checking of election and voting results;
- handwriting recognition in historical documents;
- semantic search across public documents and legal texts;
- simplifying administrative texts;
- meeting transcription;
- metadata extraction from large document collections;
- support for triaging parliamentary or chancellery requests;
- chatbot access to open data.

Why it matters:
- This is one of the clearest examples of a subnational government making AI use **visible, categorized, and discussable**.
- It is particularly useful for teaching **transparency**, **administrative use cases**, and the difference between low-stakes support tools and more sensitive algorithmic systems.

## 3. Zurich public-facing chatbot cases

### ZüriCityGPT

Source:
- [ZüriCityGPT](https://algorithmwatch.ch/en/atlas-db/zuricitygpt/) (AlgorithmWatch CH Atlas of Automation, accessed 2026-05)

Key points:
- ZüriCityGPT is described as a chatbot answering Zurich-related public questions using official city websites.
- It provides links to the sources it used.
- AlgorithmWatch classifies it in the **public sector** and notes that the developer was **Liip**.

Why it matters:
- This is a useful contrast to ZüriA.
- ZüriA is an **internal administrative assistant**, while ZüriCityGPT is a **public-facing information interface**.
- Together they show that public-sector AI adoption is not one thing: it includes both back-office augmentation and front-end citizen communication.

## 4. Federal Switzerland: coordination is becoming institutionalized

### Federal AI strategy and competence network

Sources:
- [Artificial intelligence](https://www.bk.admin.ch/bk/en/home/digitale-transformation-ikt-lenkung/kuenstliche_intelligenz.html) (Federal Chancellery, accessed 2026-05)
- [Competence Network for Artificial Intelligence](https://www.bk.admin.ch/bk/en/home/digitale-transformation-ikt-lenkung/kuenstliche_intelligenz/kinetzwerk.html) (Federal Chancellery, accessed 2026-05)
- [SB021 - Strategie Einsatz von KI-Systemen in der Bundesverwaltung](https://www.bk.admin.ch/bk/de/home/digitale-transformation-ikt-lenkung/vorgaben/sb021-strategie-einsatz-von-ki-systemen-in-der-bundesverwaltung.html) (Federal Chancellery, 2025-12-11 page version)

Key points:
- Switzerland still does **not** have a single overarching AI law; the federal government is preparing a consultation draft for AI rules by the **end of 2026**.
- The Federal Chancellery says the Confederation has a strategy and implementation plan for AI use in the federal administration.
- The **Competence Network for Artificial Intelligence** is now a formal coordination mechanism.
- The federal AI contact point moved to the **Federal Chancellery's Digital Transformation and ICT Steering Division (BK-DTI)** on **2026-02-01**.
- The network maintains a **project database** of AI-related federal-administration projects.

Why it matters:
- This is important evidence that AI in government is creating **new coordination work**, not just new software tools.
- It also supports your broader course claim: public institutions are trying to catch up by building governance structures after AI systems have already become widespread.

## 5. SwissGPT and sovereignty framing

Source:
- [Introduction of SwissGPT](https://digital.swiss/en/action-plan/measures/introduction-of-swissgpt) (Digital Switzerland Strategy action-plan measure, accessed 2026-05)

Key points:
- SwissGPT is presented as an alternative to GPT portals operated from the U.S. or China.
- The page emphasizes **Swiss operation**, **own data centers**, **data protection**, and independence from Microsoft or other foreign providers.
- The public milestones include:
  - pilot testing in 2025,
  - scaling to selected public authorities in **Q4 2025**,
  - a possible nationwide recommendation in **2026**.

Why it matters:
- This is a strong case of **digital sovereignty rhetoric**.
- It is especially useful if you want students to compare locally hosted "national" AI infrastructure with reliance on U.S.-based frontier-model providers.
