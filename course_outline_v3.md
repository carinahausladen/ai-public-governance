# AI and Public Governance

**Core question:** How should governments, public institutions, and civil society govern, deploy, and respond to increasingly capable AI systems?

This is a politics and public-administration course first, and an AI course second.

## Course premise

A recurring claim of this course is that, in contemporary AI politics, **private companies often drive change first and states respond later**. New models, products, partnerships, access programs, and safety frameworks are usually introduced by firms on their own timeline. Governments then react through procurement, regulation, litigation, guidance, public controversy, or institutional catch-up.

Students will therefore study AI governance from two directions at once:

- from the side of the **state**: regulation, procurement, public services, legitimacy, and institutional design;
- from the side of **AI companies**: product launches, public messaging, partnership strategies, safety claims, and self-governance.

The goal is not to assume that companies are always more powerful than states, but to examine when they set the pace, when states resist, and when public institutions can still shape outcomes.

---

## Proseminar II profile

This version is designed as a **Proseminar II**. Alongside the substantive study of AI and public governance, students develop an individual research project that culminates in a short exposé and a detailed exposé.

The course therefore places particular emphasis on:

- formulating a clear research question;
- locating that question in an academic and policy debate;
- developing a conceptually grounded expectation or tentative hypothesis where appropriate;
- designing a feasible empirical project.

To strengthen the learning goal of **data collection and analysis**, students work with a small corpus of documents or a small comparative set of cases drawn from the course materials, especially the media digest, the lab-source files, and the Switzerland case.

---

## Course materials

This course uses two kinds of sources.

### 1. Lab-source files in [`sources/`](sources/)

These are primary documents of corporate self-presentation: product announcements, partnership pages, public-benefit claims, and lab-linked research. They are useful not because they are neutral, but because they show how powerful private actors present their own role in public life.

### 2. Current-affairs digest in [`sources/current_media_digest.md`](sources/current_media_digest.md)

This is the course's live media dataset. It captures recent newspaper coverage and selected platform discourse around AI and public governance. It helps students track which issues become public controversies, how the state is framed in practice, and where institutions seem to be reacting rather than leading.

### 3. Switzerland case note in [`sources/switzerland_public_sector_ai.md`](sources/switzerland_public_sector_ai.md)

This is a focused country case on Swiss public-sector AI. It is especially useful because it connects city-level deployment, cantonal transparency, federal coordination, sovereignty rhetoric, and the emergence of new implementation and governance roles.

---

## Learning objectives

By the end of the course, students should be able to:

1. Explain what modern AI systems can and cannot do.
2. Analyze how AI affects public policy, administration, and democratic politics.
3. Evaluate how governments procure, deploy, and regulate AI systems.
4. Compare public regulation, corporate self-governance, and hybrid governance arrangements.
5. Critically interpret claims made by AI developers and distinguish evidence from positioning.
6. Design practical governance responses to real AI-related public problems.
7. Translate a substantive course question into a feasible small empirical research design using documents, media sources, or comparative cases.

## Module 1: Foundations — What AI Can and Cannot Do (Weeks 1–2)

**Guiding question:** What exactly is being governed?

Topics:

* Capabilities and limits of modern AI systems
* Augmentation vs. automation
* How capability claims are made
* How media narratives differ from lab narratives

Key materials:

* [05 Societal & economic impacts](sources/05-societal-and-economic-impacts.md)

* [Current media digest](sources/current_media_digest.md)
* [Switzerland: AI in the public sector](sources/switzerland_public_sector_ai.md)

Teaching emphasis:

Students learn to distinguish technical possibility from observed use, and hype from evidence. This module gives them the conceptual baseline needed for the rest of the course.

## Module 2: AI and the State (Weeks 3–4)

**Guiding question:** How does the state adopt AI, and on whose terms?

Topics:

* Public-sector adoption
* Digital government
* Procurement
* Administrative capacity
* AI and austerity
* Vendor dependence and cloud intermediation

Cases:

* Tax administration
* Benefits administration
* Immigration systems
* Public health
* GSA x OpenAI procurement deal
* ChatGPT Gov / OpenAI for Government
* Public-sector pilot and deployment cases
* **Switzerland case cluster**: ZüriA, ZüriCityGPT, Canton Zurich AI projects, and the federal AI competence network

Media overlay:

* Federal AI adoption in the U.S. government
* New Zealand public-sector cuts tied to AI uptake
* IRS capacity under workforce pressure
* OpenAI access to agencies through AWS
* Switzerland: local hosting, cantonal transparency, and federal coordination

Teaching emphasis:

This module treats the state not only as regulator, but as customer, deployer, and institutional actor under pressure. A key theme is that governments often adopt tools built elsewhere, under terms shaped by vendors, cloud providers, and security standards they do not fully control.

The Switzerland material adds an important variation: sometimes the public sector tries to regain control by building local infrastructure, publishing project inventories, and creating coordination mechanisms of its own.

## Module 3: AI and Democracy (Weeks 5–6)

**Guiding question:** What happens when AI enters elections, public communication, and the information environment?

Topics:

* Elections
* Misinformation
* Political persuasion
* Civic participation
* Election administration
* Platform governance
* Authenticity and public trust

Cases:

* Election-related AI deployments
* Deepfakes
* Government communication
* Chatbot misinformation in elections
* Deepfake detection pilots

Teaching emphasis:

This module focuses on the democratic consequences of generative AI: not just false content, but institutional credibility, voter reliance on AI tools, and the difficulty of governing political communication when technical systems move faster than election law.

Exercise:

Students evaluate a hypothetical election commission's AI policy.

---

## Module 4: AI Policy and Regulation (Weeks 7–8)

**Guiding question:** Who governs AI, and through what kinds of rules?

Topics:

* Risk-based regulation
* The EU AI Act
* U.S. approaches
* International governance
* Federalism and pre-emption
* Implementation politics
* Compliance as governance practice

Key entities:

* OECD
* United Nations
* European Commission

Teaching emphasis:

Students compare binding regulation, soft law, executive action, procurement-based governance, and corporate self-regulation. The point is not only to study formal rules, but also to see how governance happens through guidance, contracts, standards, and compliance language.

Switzerland is a helpful comparison case here because it is not centered on a single EU-style AI Act. Instead, it combines guidelines, coordination, sectoral regulation, and gradual institutional build-out.

Assignment anchor:

Compare three governance frameworks, for example:

* the EU AI Act,
* a U.S. executive or procurement-centered approach,
* a frontier lab's self-governance model.

## Module 5: AI for Public Good (Weeks 9–10)

**Guiding question:** When AI is framed as socially beneficial, who defines the benefit?

Topics:

* Education
* Healthcare
* Scientific discovery
* Public services
* Nonprofit applications
* Distribution of benefits

Materials:

* [01 Education](sources/01-education.md)
* [03 Nonprofits and public good](sources/03-nonprofits-and-public-good.md)
* [05 Societal & economic impacts](sources/05-societal-and-economic-impacts.md)

Teaching emphasis:

This module is intentionally more skeptical than celebratory. Students examine whether "AI for public good" means genuine social benefit, targeted philanthropy, reputational strategy, agenda-setting, or some mixture of all three.

---

## Module 6: Future Governance (Weeks 11–12)

**Guiding question:** What institutions do we need if companies continue to move first?

Topics:

* Frontier AI governance
* National AI strategies
* Compute governance
* Institutional design
* Civil-military AI governance
* Contracts as governance tools

Materials:

* [04 Policy, governance, and public benefit](sources/04-policy-governance-and-public-benefit.md)
* [05 Societal & economic impacts](sources/05-societal-and-economic-impacts.md)
* [Current media digest](sources/current_media_digest.md)
* [Switzerland: AI in the public sector](sources/switzerland_public_sector_ai.md)

Teaching emphasis:

This final module asks students to move from critique to design. If firms can launch systems, shape expectations, and create dependencies before states act, what institutions would let democratic publics govern more proactively rather than reactively?

The Swiss case is especially useful here because it shows a state trying to move from scattered experimentation to more stable coordination capacity.

Final question:

> What institutions should exist in 2035 that do not exist today?

## Deliverables

### 1. Weekly Frontier AI Governance Tracker

Each week, students monitor the public output of major AI companies and institutions, especially:

* [OpenAI Newsroom](https://openai.com/news/)
* [Anthropic News](https://www.anthropic.com/news)
* [OECD AI Observatory](https://oecd.ai)
* [Stanford HAI](https://hai.stanford.edu)

At least twice during the term, students should also include one **country case** entry. Switzerland is the recommended starting case because it shows how public institutions react not only through law, but through infrastructure, staffing, and administrative coordination.

Each tracker entry must include:

* one development;
* one governance implication;
* one policy recommendation;
* one critical question about framing;
* one source type: `lab self-presentation`, `newspaper reporting`, `policy institution`, or `platform post`.

Format:

* one entry per week;
* approximately 250 to 350 words per entry;
* students should rotate across company sources, policy institutions, and media coverage over the term.

Purpose:

This is the course's running empirical dataset. It trains students to observe how companies publicly frame AI, how quickly those framings travel, and how states and public institutions respond. It also gives students a shared source base from which they can develop their own exposés.

### 2. Presentation / Project Colloquium

Students give a short presentation on their developing project.

The presentation should:

* introduce the research question;
* locate the project in a debate covered by the course;
* explain the chosen case or corpus;
* identify the planned data or materials and the mode of analysis;
* invite concrete feedback from peers.

Format:

* 10 to 15 minutes.

One or two sessions can be organized as project colloquia in which students present their short exposés and receive peer feedback.

### 3. Short Exposé / Project Sketch

Students submit a short exposé or outline for their seminar paper.

The exposé should include:

* the research question;
* the motivation and research gap;
* a tentative conceptual framework and, where appropriate, a working expectation or hypothesis;
* the chosen case or corpus;
* the planned data or materials;
* the analysis strategy;
* a short bibliography.

Format:

* approximately 2 pages plus bibliography.

Possible project pathways include:

* a live governance controversy;
* a comparative governance analysis;
* an institutional design project grounded in empirical cases rather than treated as pure normative speculation.

### 4. Detailed Exposé / Seminar Paper

Students write a detailed exposé that develops the research question, conceptual framework, and empirical design in full.

The paper should:

* elaborate the research question and its relevance;
* situate the project in the literature or debate;
* explain the case or corpus selection;
* specify how data or materials will be collected and analyzed;
* discuss feasibility, limitations, and expected contribution.

Format:

* 10 to 12 pages;
* students should draw on at least two course modules and, where useful, the weekly tracker as an empirical source base.

## Recurring analytic questions

Throughout the course, students should keep returning to a small set of questions:

* Who is moving first here: the state, the company, or another actor?
* Is the state shaping the field, or reacting to a field shaped by firms?
* Who benefits from the current arrangement?
* What dependencies are being created?
* What kind of governance is actually happening: law, procurement, standards, publicity, or self-regulation?

These questions tie the modules together and keep the course focused on public power, institutional timing, and the politics of reaction versus initiative.
