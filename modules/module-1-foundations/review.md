# Module 1 Review

## Foundations — What AI Can and Cannot Do

Module 1 gives students the conceptual vocabulary needed for the rest of the course.

The most important lesson is that AI politics should not begin with vague claims about disruption. It should begin with sharper distinctions:

- capability is not the same as adoption;
- exposure is not the same as displacement;
- augmentation is not the same as automation;
- productivity is not the same as public benefit.

Week 1 establishes that modern AI systems may have broad task-level relevance, but real-world uptake remains uneven. This helps students avoid both technological determinism and complacency.

Week 2 then shows that once AI enters real institutions, the effects are mixed. Some tasks are sped up, some are partially delegated, some become easier for less experienced workers, and some remain contested because efficiency is not the only value at stake.

Across both weeks, a second lesson also emerges: companies are not just building tools. They are also shaping the language through which society understands those tools. Scientific papers, usage reports, and institute announcements all help define what counts as capability, impact, and responsible governance.

That is why this module matters so much for the rest of the syllabus. Before students can debate procurement, democracy, regulation, or public good, they need to learn how to read AI claims critically and how to distinguish empirical evidence from institutional positioning.

## Main concepts students should retain

- technical capability
- observed adoption
- task exposure
- augmentation
- automation
- productivity effects
- corporate framing

## Transition to Module 2

If Module 1 asks what AI can do, Module 2 asks what happens when the state starts to use it.

The shift is important. Once AI moves from possibility to public adoption, the central questions become institutional:

- Who procures the system?
- Who controls the infrastructure?
- Who is accountable when things go wrong?
- Does the state gain capacity, or does it become more dependent on private vendors?
