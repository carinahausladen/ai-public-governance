# Module 1, Week 2

## Augmentation vs. automation; reading capability claims

**Guiding question:** When AI enters real work and education, does it replace people, support them, or reorganize what good work looks like?

## Core takeaway

The key distinction this week is between **augmentation** and **automation**. AI does not simply "take over" tasks. In many settings it speeds work up, redistributes expertise, changes workflows, and alters what human contribution is expected to look like.

## Scientific anchor

### Experimental Evidence on the Productivity Effects of Generative Artificial Intelligence

Noy and Zhang provide one of the clearest early experimental studies of generative AI in practice. In their experiment, ChatGPT improved productivity on professional writing tasks: participants completed tasks faster and produced better outputs on average.

For this course, the most important point is not just that productivity rose. It is that the gains were not evenly distributed. Lower-performing workers benefited more, which suggests that AI can sometimes compress skill gaps rather than widen them.

This makes the paper especially useful for public governance because it opens two competing interpretations:

- optimistic: AI can democratize access to higher-quality work;
- skeptical: AI may standardize performance while shifting dependence toward a small number of tool providers.

The paper is therefore a good bridge between workplace productivity and later debates about education, public services, and inequality.

## Lab and source notes

### Anthropic Education Report: How Educators Use Claude

Anthropic's education report is valuable because it shows augmentation and automation playing out differently across academic tasks.

The strongest pattern is that student-facing, interpretive, and creative tasks tend to remain more augmentative, while routine administrative tasks lean more heavily toward automation. That suggests AI is not affecting all institutional labor in the same way.

The report also shows that even where automation is technically possible, organizations do not always welcome it. Grading, for example, appears highly automatable in the usage data, but faculty remain skeptical of it as a good pedagogical use.

That tension is important for the course: technical feasibility does not settle the normative question of where AI should be used.

### Introducing The Anthropic Institute

This reading is not an empirical study of workplace productivity. Its value lies elsewhere. It is a strong example of how a frontier lab narrates rapid capability progress and tries to position itself as a legitimate producer of public-interest knowledge.

The Institute presents powerful AI as a near-term societal transformation and frames Anthropic as a serious actor thinking about employment, resilience, governance, and values. Students should read that in two ways:

- as a statement about where the lab thinks the world is heading;
- as a corporate claim to epistemic and political authority.

This is exactly the kind of text that helps students practice critical reading in this course.

## Why this week matters politically

Week 2 moves the course from abstract capability to real organizational effects. It shows that the governance question is not simply whether AI works. The deeper question is where AI should augment human judgment, where it should automate routine work, and who gets to decide.

This is where the course's politics-first lens becomes visible. Productivity gains are never just technical facts. They become politically meaningful when they affect labor, professional authority, education, accountability, and dependency on private systems.

## Questions to carry forward

- When does augmentation become de facto automation?
- Who benefits when AI narrows performance gaps: workers, institutions, or vendors?
- How should public institutions evaluate AI systems that improve efficiency but alter professional judgment?
