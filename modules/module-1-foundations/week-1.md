# Module 1, Week 1

## Capabilities, limits, and the pace of change

**Guiding question:** What can modern AI systems actually do, and how should we talk about their impact without confusing potential with reality?

## Core takeaway

This week introduces a crucial distinction for the whole course: AI systems may be **capable** of affecting many tasks, but that does not mean they are already **widely adopted**, **institutionally embedded**, or **politically governed** in the same way across sectors.

## Scientific anchor

### GPTs are GPTs: An Early Look at the Labor Market Impact Potential of Large Language Models

The paper by Eloundou, Manning, Mishkin, and Rock asks how exposed different occupations are to large language models. Its main value for this course is conceptual. It does not claim that jobs will disappear immediately. Instead, it maps where LLMs could plausibly change work tasks.

The key argument is that LLMs behave like a potentially general-purpose technology. Their effects are not confined to one narrow sector, and higher-income, text-heavy occupations may be more exposed than many people initially assumed. That matters politically because it pushes AI governance beyond the familiar story of automation hitting only routine, low-wage work.

For teaching purposes, the paper helps students separate three things:

- technical capability;
- task exposure;
- real-world institutional adoption.

That distinction should stay with them for the rest of the semester.

## Lab and source notes

### Anthropic: Labor Market Impacts of AI

Anthropic's labor-market report adds an important corrective to pure exposure claims. It introduces the idea of **observed exposure**, which tries to connect theoretical model capability to actual usage data.

The most important finding is that real adoption still lags behind technical possibility. In other words, much more may be feasible than is currently happening in practice. That is a central governance insight: public debate often reacts to frontier capability, while real institutions are still unevenly adopting and integrating these systems.

The report also identifies strong occupational variation. Programmers, customer-service representatives, and data-entry workers appear highly exposed, while many manual and embodied occupations remain much less exposed. This gives students a more grounded way to discuss who is likely to feel AI change first.

### Anthropic Economic Index

The Economic Index is useful less for one single headline result than for the type of project it is. It treats AI adoption as something to be **measured empirically over time**, not simply predicted in abstract terms.

That matters for a public-governance course because it suggests that governing AI well requires institutions that can monitor diffusion, task change, and sectoral differences, not only institutions that write general principles.

## Why this week matters politically

This week gives students a baseline for resisting two bad habits:

- overstating what AI is already doing everywhere;
- understating how broad the potential scope of change may be.

The politics of AI often swings between hype and dismissal. Week 1 should help students stay in the more useful middle ground: AI is already significant, but its effects are uneven, mediated, and institutionally shaped.

## Questions to carry forward

- Which matters more for governance: what AI can do in theory, or what organizations are actually adopting?
- If exposure is broad but adoption is uneven, where should governments focus first?
- How should public institutions monitor AI diffusion without simply echoing corporate claims?
