AI and Public Governance

How should governments, public institutions, and civil society govern, deploy, and respond to increasingly capable AI systems?

This course is designed as a politics and public-administration seminar first, and an AI course second. It treats AI governance not only as a question of regulation, but also of procurement, public services, democratic legitimacy, and institutional design.

Course premise

Private AI companies often move first and states respond later.

New models, products, partnerships, access programs, and safety frameworks are usually introduced by firms on their own timeline. Governments then react through procurement, regulation, litigation, guidance, public controversy, or institutional catch-up. The course tests that claim across cases rather than assuming it is always true.

Proseminar II profile

Substantive AI politics with a clear research-design spine

The course preserves a strong public-governance focus while giving students structured support for research-question development, theory building, case selection, and empirical design.

Research question

Students move from a broad course theme toward a precise, workable research question anchored in current governance debates.

Debate and gap

Each project is located in an academic or policy conversation and framed around a clearly identifiable problem, tension, or gap.

Data and analysis

To strengthen data collection and analysis, students work with a document corpus, a media dataset, or a small comparative case set.

Writing progression

The seminar culminates in a short exposé, a project colloquium, and a detailed exposé that develops the research design in full.

Course arc

Six modules, moving from capability claims to institutional design

The seminar begins by clarifying what is actually being governed, then tracks AI across the state, democracy, regulation, public-good rhetoric, and future institutional arrangements.

Weeks 1–2

Module 1
Foundations

What exactly is being governed?

Capabilities and limits of modern AI systems, augmentation versus automation, and how lab narratives differ from empirical evidence.

Weeks 3–4

Module 2
AI and the State

How does the state adopt AI, and on whose terms?

Public-sector adoption, digital government, procurement, vendor dependence, administrative capacity, and the Swiss public-sector case.

Weeks 5–6

Module 3
AI and Democracy

What happens when AI enters elections and public communication?

Misinformation, political persuasion, deepfakes, election administration, platform governance, and public trust.

Weeks 7–8

Module 4
AI Policy and Regulation

Who governs AI, and through what kinds of rules?

Risk-based regulation, the EU AI Act, U.S. approaches, executive action, procurement-based governance, and implementation politics.

Weeks 9–10

Module 5
AI for Public Good

When AI is framed as socially beneficial, who defines the benefit?

Education, nonprofit applications, distributional questions, and the politics of public-benefit narratives.

Weeks 11–12

Module 6
Future Governance

What institutions do we need if companies continue to move first?

Frontier governance, civil-military bargaining, national strategies, contracts as governance tools, and institutional design.

Weekly rhythm

Representative weekly topics

Each week pairs scientific literature with policy documents, journalism, and selected primary corporate materials.

01

Capabilities, limits, and the pace of change

Students distinguish technical capability, task exposure, and institutional adoption.

02

Augmentation versus automation

The course reads productivity claims critically and asks where AI should support judgment rather than replace it.

03

Public-sector adoption and digital government

Cases include OpenAI for Government, U.S. federal adoption, and Zurich's secure internal AI assistant.

04

Procurement, vendor dependence, and capacity

Students examine cloud intermediation, bargaining power, and the administrative politics of AI uptake.

05

Misinformation and elections

A democracy module focused on election information, deepfakes, and institutional response.

06

Political persuasion and government communication

The seminar moves beyond falsehood to legitimacy, affect, and authenticity in public communication.

07

Risk-based regulation and the EU AI Act

Students compare law on the books with compliance discourse and ongoing political contestation.

08

U.S. approaches, federalism, and self-regulation

Public regulation, executive action, and frontier-lab governance are set into direct comparison.

09

Education and the public-good claim

AI in higher education becomes a test case for benefit, pedagogy, access, and organizational strategy.

10

Nonprofits and the distribution of benefits

Students ask whether claims of social benefit describe real redistribution, strategic philanthropy, or agenda-setting.

11

Institutional design and lab self-governance

The seminar examines whether current public-interest and trust models are politically credible.

12

Frontier governance and contracts as governance tools

The final week asks what institutions should exist by 2035 that do not exist today.

Empirical pathways

Three feasible routes into data collection and analysis

The course is designed so that students can complete an empirical project without turning the seminar into a full methods course.

Document and discourse analysis

Compare how labs, governments, and journalism frame issues such as safety, sovereignty, public benefit, or administrative efficiency.

Small comparative case design

Analyze two to four public-sector AI cases across variables such as governance mechanism, transparency, or vendor dependence.

Institutional design grounded in evidence

Develop a governance proposal that is explicitly anchored in empirical cases rather than treated as pure normative speculation.

Deliverables

Writing and research progression across the term

Weekly observation, project discussion, and staged exposé writing support the Proseminar II format.

Weekly frontier AI governance tracker

250–350 words per week

Each entry identifies one development, one governance implication, one policy recommendation, one framing question, and one source type.

Presentation / project colloquium

10–15 minutes

Students present their question, case or corpus, and planned mode of analysis, then receive structured peer feedback.

Short exposé / project sketch

Approx. 2 pages + bibliography

The short exposé introduces the research question, motivation, conceptual frame, chosen materials, and analysis strategy.

Detailed exposé / seminar paper

10–12 pages

The final paper develops the research question, debate, case or corpus selection, data collection, and analysis plan in full.

Shared source base

Course materials already organized for empirical use

The course draws on a deliberately mixed source base that helps students compare scientific evidence, public documents, journalism, and corporate self-presentation.

Lab-source files

Primary documents of corporate self-presentation: product announcements, partnership pages, lab research, and public-benefit claims.

Current-affairs digest

A structured teaching dataset of recent media and practitioner discourse around AI and public governance.

Switzerland case note

A focused public-sector case linking city-level deployment, cantonal transparency, federal coordination, and sovereignty rhetoric.
